# LinkedIn — 2026-04-05

Milwaukee already has the data. What it didn't have was a front door.

I spent the weekend building one.

The MKE Neighborhood Vitality Dashboard now covers 8 northside neighborhoods — Lindsay Heights, Harambee, Amani, Metcalfe Park, and four others — with 30+ data points each pulled from 15 live sources: Milwaukee's ArcGIS REST services, the CKAN open data portal, MPROP property records, and Census ACS.

Day 2 of building and it now speaks four languages. English, Spanish, Hmong, Arabic. Every metric label — "Vacant Buildings," "Median Home Value," "311 Requests" — routes through a translation pipeline. Milwaukee has one of the largest Hmong communities in the country. The dashboard should reflect that from the start, not as an afterthought.

What changed the story today: economic data. Before I wired in property sales and liquor licenses, Harambee was "66 foreclosures." After: "66 foreclosures, 100 property sales at a median of $138,000." Those are different stories. Distress and investment coexisting. The dashboard now shows both.

The data model for connecting it was interesting — property sales don't have coordinates, they have taxkeys. MPROP parcels have both. So we join across two databases (ArcGIS and CKAN) using taxkey as the bridge. Civic data is messy. You use whatever key connects the dots.

The 1930s HOLC redlining overlay sits underneath all of it. That's not decoration. Milwaukee's northside neighborhoods were redlined. The vacancy and disinvestment visible in 2026 data isn't random — it has a map, and the map is 90 years old.

Still early. The sync pipeline needs optimization. The Hmong translations need a fluent reviewer. A Housing tab probably needs to be its own category.

But the core is there. If you're doing civic data, community development, or urban planning work in Milwaukee, I'd like to show you what we've built.
